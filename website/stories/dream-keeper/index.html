27–29 April 2023 · The Crossing

On the afternoon of 27 April 2023, the liveaboard dive yacht M/V Dream Keeper left San Remigio at the northern tip of Cebu and turned toward the open Sulu Sea. On board were 32 people — 12 guests, 15 crew and five divemasters — bound for the dive trip every Filipino diver dreams about: Tubbataha.

The crossing takes the better part of two days. The Dream Keeper reached the reef about 10 p.m. on Saturday, 29 April, anchoring in open water for the first dives of the morning. Tubbataha allows visitors only in a short season — roughly mid-March to mid-June — the months when the Sulu Sea is supposed to be at its calmest.

15 M

30 April · 6:49 A.M.

Supposed to be. At dawn on Sunday a squall — a sudden, violent burst of wind and rain that these seas give almost no warning of — caught the yacht at anchor. The Dream Keeper capsized.

At 6:49 a.m. the Philippine Coast Guard command center received the report from Coast Guard District Palawan: a dive boat was going down at the edge of the country's most protected waters.

A squall is over in minutes. What it leaves behind is not.

The Rescue

Twenty-eight of the thirty-two came out of the water alive, among them four Chinese tourists. Four Filipinos did not: a divemaster, two passengers — and the owner of the Dream Keeper, who had sailed with his own boat.

1 May · The Search Widens

On 1 May the Philippine Coast Guard confirmed what the survivors already knew: the Dream Keeper had capsized in a sudden squall. It added that an oil slick had been spotted on the sea near where the liveaboard sank — some four nautical miles from the World Heritage-listed marine sanctuary of Tubbataha Reef. The PCG monitored the slick with a response team on standby.

Above the wreck site, the search grew into one of the largest the Sulu Sea had seen in years. The Armed Forces Western Command reported that search and rescue intensified on 1 May, with a Britten-Norman Islander, a US Navy P-8 Poseidon and two V-22 Osprey aircraft taking turns sweeping the water. A second naval vessel, BRP Felix Apolinario, was dispatched to join BRP Carlos Albert on scene, and the possibility of launching a naval helicopter from the coast guard patrol ship BRP Melchora Aquino was being explored.

The Reef · Tubbataha

The sanctuary the oil slick drifted toward is the crown of Philippine seas. Tubbataha — from the Sama language, "long reef exposed at low tide" — sits alone in the middle of the Sulu Sea, about 150 kilometers southeast of Puerto Princesa, Palawan: two coral atolls and the Jessie Beazley Reef, almost 100,000 hectares of protected water with no permanent human settlement except a ranger station on stilts.

Inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1993 — among the first in the Philippines — the park is a no-take marine sanctuary whose reef walls drop 100 meters straight into the deep. Scientists have recorded around 600 species of fish, 360 species of coral — roughly half of all known coral species — 11 species of shark, 13 species of dolphins and whales, and 100 species of birds; hawksbill and green sea turtles nest on its islets.

That is why an oil sheen four nautical miles out put rangers, divers and the coast guard on alert at once: everything in this stretch of sea — the fisheries of the whole Sulu Sea system, the dive season, the reputation of Philippine diving itself — flows outward from this one reef.

The People of These Waters · Batang Dagat

Long before the Dream Keeper made the news, waters like these belonged — in the way that matters — to people like Batang Dagat: "children of the sea." A couple, photographers, who have worked with Budots Media on productions in our home waters off Cebu.

They shoot the way freediving image-makers do: on a single breath, no tanks, no bubbles between the lens and the animal, surfacing to breathe and going down again — and their frames carry that particular deep, smooth blue that Filipino underwater photographers have made a signature. For that community, Tubbataha is the pilgrimage: two days of open sea for a few perfect days below it, booked a season in advance on boats exactly like the Dream Keeper.

When a liveaboard goes down at the reef's doorstep, it is not an abstract headline to the children of the sea. It is their boat, their season, their sea.

It is the same sea our own cameras live on — the waters where we filmed the Little Merman swim around Mactan and the outrigger canoes cross channels at dawn.
